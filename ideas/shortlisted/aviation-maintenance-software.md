## One-Line Pitch
Paperless maintenance tracking for small Part 145 repair stations — digital mechanic sign-offs, AD compliance tracking, and FAA-ready audit reports without a 1990s desktop app.

## Problem

General aviation maintenance shops (Part 145 FAA-certified repair stations) are legally required to maintain detailed maintenance records for every aircraft they service: work orders, airworthiness directive (AD) compliance, mechanic certifications, parts traceability. Today, most shops do this on:
- Paper logbooks and binders
- 20-year-old Windows desktop software (CAMP Systems — targets large fleets, not 2–10 mechanic shops)
- Disconnected spreadsheets

HN insider with direct aviation maintenance experience confirmed: "existing software serves large MROs, not small shops." An FAA violation from poor recordkeeping costs orders of magnitude more than any software subscription.

**The regulatory moat**: FAA compliance is not optional. This is non-discretionary spend with built-in urgency. Once a shop's records are in a system, they virtually never switch mid-inspection cycle.

## Market Evidence

- ~3,500 FAA Part 145 certificated repair stations in the US (small airport FBOs, standalone GA maintenance shops)
- Global aviation MRO market: $88B; small GA segment specifically underserved
- CAMP Systems, Corridor, Quantum MX serve large commercial fleets; no modern cloud option for 2–10 mechanic Part 145 shops
- HN "underserved industries" thread: commenter with direct aviation maintenance experience identified this gap
- AOPA has 400,000+ members; many own or fly aircraft requiring maintenance at small shops

## Key Differentiators
1. **Cloud-native** — no Windows install, no on-premise server; access from any device including tablet on the shop floor
2. **Digital mechanic sign-offs** — legally compliant with FAA Part 145; eliminates paper logbooks
3. **AD compliance tracker** — automatically tracks airworthiness directives per aircraft type (York, Cessna, Piper, Beechcraft); alerts when AD work is due
4. **Audit-ready export** — one-click PDF of work orders + AD compliance history for FAA audits
5. **Parts traceability** — links installed parts to specific work orders with certificate of conformance (COC) documentation
6. **Mobile** — mechanics sign off on tablet/phone; no printing, no "lost the paperwork" scenarios

## LTD Pricing
- Not recommended — compliance monitoring requires ongoing subscription
- **$299/month per shop** (2–10 mechanics)
- Annual plan at $2,499 (30% discount) = better conversion for budget-conscious shop owners

## Target Channels

- EAA (Experimental Aircraft Association) — AirVenture Oshkosh attendance

- Sun 'n Fun Aerospace Expo (Lakeland, FL) — premier GA maintenance gathering
- MX-Aid, AvWeb, General Aviation News — trade publications

## Next Steps
1. Domain expert validation: interview 5–10 Part 145 mechanics/shop owners at local GA airports about current workflow and specific FAA recordkeeping pain; offer 3-month free beta
2. Build MVP: digital maintenance log (work order → mechanic sign-off → completion) + AD compliance checklist per aircraft type + parts traceability + PDF audit export
3. Start with piston GA aircraft (Cessna, Piper, Beechcraft) — most common; ADs are well-documented
4. Price $299/month; target 20 beta customers from GA airport cold outreach (walk in and ask)

## Top Risks
1. **FAA liability** — errors in compliance tracking could expose the tool (and shop) to FAA violations; requires clear disclaimers and legal review; must not claim to replace mechanic judgment
2. **Domain expertise required** — cannot be built correctly without someone who understands Part 145 regulations, ADs, Form 8130, 8643, etc.
3. **Small TAM** — ~3,500 Part 145 shops in the US; $10K MRR requires only 34 customers at $299/mo; ceiling is real
4. **Long sales cycle** — shop owners deeply cautious about changing FAA-regulated records systems; expect 3–6 month sales cycles
5. **International expansion** — EASA Part-145 (Europe) has similar but distinct requirements; add complexity
