## One-Line Pitch
Farm record-keeping and USDA compliance software sold as a lifetime deal — uniquely suited to farmers because tax law favors capital expenditures over SaaS subscriptions.

## Problem
Farmers rely heavily on spreadsheets despite having ag economics degrees. The key blocker most SaaS founders miss: **US tax law favors capital expenditures over recurring software subscriptions** — farmers prefer to expense a one-time software purchase rather than deducting an ongoing subscription. This is the only major market segment where LTD pricing is a regulatory/tax advantage, not just a marketing tactic.

Beyond buyer psychology, there's a real compliance gap. Farmers participating in USDA programs (crop insurance, organic certification, USDA subsidy programs) must document practices: field-by-field crop records, input application logs, yield histories, equipment maintenance records, and labor logs for GAP (Good Agricultural Practices) audits. Most do this in binders and Excel. State extension services acknowledge the gap — they just don't build the software.

## Market Evidence
- Mid-size farms (50-2,000 acres) are the sweet spot — large enough to need documentation but too small for enterprise ag ERP
- Crop insurance (federally subsidized) requires documented production records per field — USDA RMA mandates
- USDA organic certification requires 3-year transition records with no synthetic inputs — a major paper burden
- GAP audits (required to sell to major grocery chains, food service) require detailed worker hygiene, field activity, and spray records
- No modern SaaS targets this specific compliance layer; legacy tools require capital expenditure budget and on-premise IT

## Key Differentiators
1. **One-time pricing** — matches farmer tax preference for capital expenditure over subscription
2. **USDA program compliance exports** — crop insurance, organic certification, USDA subsidy reporting
3. **Field-level record keeping** — per-field crop records, input application logs, yield history
4. **GAP audit readiness** — worker hygiene, spray records, field activity logs in one place
5. **Offline-capable** — farms may have poor connectivity

## MVP Scope (4-6 weeks)
1. Field/plot registry (acreage, crop type, GPS boundaries)
2. Input application log (fertilizer, pesticide, irrigation — date, rate, applicator)
3. Harvest records by field (yield, moisture, grade)
4. Equipment maintenance log (tractor service, implement hours)
5. USDA crop insurance production history report (exportable)
6. GAP audit checklist with documentation attachments

## Go-to-Market
1. University extension program blogs and newsletters (free channel)
2. USDA Farm Service Agency (FSA) county offices — they see farmers in compliance trouble
3. Agricultural lending officers (banks that write farm loans) — compliance records = bankability
4. Farm Bureau member networks
5. Farm accounting software communities (farmers using FarmBooks, QuickBooks Farm)

## Risks
1. Farmers resistant to any digital adoption — even free tools struggle for adoption
2. USDA program rules change annually — ongoing compliance maintenance required
3. Market TAM is smaller than other targets; MRR path requires premium upgrade hooks
4. Seasonal cash flow — farm software purchases cluster in fall/winter off-season
